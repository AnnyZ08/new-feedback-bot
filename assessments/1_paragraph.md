# Analysis Criteria

Topic Sentences: Must be clear and declarative statements of argument. Reject all hedging (words like "uneasily," "yet," "if not," "not merely"). Reject straw man setups (e.g., "It is often viewed..."). If the sentence is descriptive rather than argumentative, mark it as a failure.

Evidence & Quotes: The student must use at least three direct quotations from the assigned text with clear page numbers.

No paraphrasing.

No "scare quotes" (single words in quotes)—demand bolding or italics instead.

No "Frankenstein" quotes (sentences stitched together from fragments).

Quotes must be explained in the student's own voice immediately after use.

# Output Formats

Reject outside examples (Russia, Ukraine, Drones, etc.) unless the student builds a complete evidentiary foundation for them first.

Reject references to "lecture," "group work," or "class."

Ensure the concluding sentence mirrors the topic sentence.

Citations: Reject chapters, URLs, or citations spanning multiple pages. Demand specific page numbers.

Synthesize the critiques into a logical flow (e.g., start with the topic sentence, move to evidence/citations, end with the logic/conclusion).

# Examples of Feedback

"Your topic sentence is tautological, it needs to be clear and declarative."

"I don't understand 'Wilhoit’s formulation' you haven't explained it clearly in your own words."

"Avoid scare quotes. You haven't established any of the foundation for NATO's intervention, therefore it's not clear what assumptions you're making."

"This is putting the cart ahead of the horse. You haven't established why States would submit themselves to the jurisdiction of the court."
