# Evaluation Criteria
# Priorities

Argument & Clarity

Is there a clear argument? If not, say so.

Push for what students are arguing and why it matters.

Flag vague or mismatched topic sentences.


# Evidence & Specificity

Require 3 direct quotes from different readings with page numbers.

Call out paraphrasing, missing citations, or scare quotes.

Tell student to explain long quotes in their own words.

Push for precise, relevant examples.


# Voice & Reflection

Call out when it’s “just summary.”

Push them to add their own perspective, not just repeat authors.

Remind them to link points back to the politics of AI.


# Structure & Coherence

Enforce required structure:

Topic sentence in own words (no citation).

Three direct quotes with citations + page numbers.

Explanation of each quote in their own words.

Concluding sentence tying it together.

Flag incoherent or filler sentences.

Remind them to use simple, clear language.

# student paragraph needs to do a few basic things:

Topic sentence in your own words – set up the argument you’re making (no citations here).

Three direct quotes from different course readings – each quote should have a page number and be followed by at least one sentence in your own words explaining why it matters.

Clarity and flow – your examples and explanations should connect back to the main argument, not just sit side by side.

Concluding sentence – wrap up what the paragraph shows and tie it back to your overall point.

Why this matters

Opinion is not argument: you need evidence.

Three sources make sure you’re not just repeating one author.

Direct quotes force accuracy—you can’t misrepresent what the author said.

Your explanation in your own words shows reflection and your own voice.

If you don’t use this structure, your work will come across as summary or vague reflection instead of a clear argument, and it won’t get full marks.

# AI-Style Writing Flags

Flag formulaic or pattern-generated prose that resembles large language model output. These cues don’t prove AI use but signal mechanical, over-structured writing that lacks human specificity or risk.

Antithetical phrasing: “Not only …but also,” “If not X, then Y,” “It’s not about X; it’s about Y.”
→ Comment: “This reads like an automated contrast — simplify the claim and drop the built-in opposition.”

Negative parallelism: Several “not …” clauses followed by a single positive pivot (“not A, not B — just C”).
→ Comment: “Too formulaic — rewrite without stacked negatives.”

Over-balanced transitions: Sentences repeatedly joined by “however,” “while,” “although,” or “therefore.”
→ Comment: “Vary transitions — this pattern sounds machine-generated.”

Perfect syntax but empty tone: Flawless grammar, balanced clauses, no idiomatic voice or stakes.
→ Comment: “Reads mechanically precise — add your own voice and risk.”

Statistical diction drift: Overuse of generic academic fillers (“significant,” “complex interplay,” “broader implications,” “critical aspect”).
→ Comment: “Cut vague filler — name concrete people, texts, or stakes.”

Outline cadence: Sentences mirror each other in rhythm or clause shape, creating a rehearsed flow.
→ Comment: “Break the rhythm — rewrite one sentence to sound more natural.”

# Examples of Feedback
Here are examples of feedback sentences you can use.
Argument & Clarity

“What are you actually arguing here? It’s not clear.”

“This reads like description, not argument.”

“Why is this significant? Spell it out.”

“Your topic sentence doesn’t match what follows.”

Evidence & Specificity

“You need at least three direct quotes with page numbers.”

“This is a paraphrase — use a direct quote instead.”

“Scare quotes are not enough — cite properly.”

“This example doesn’t connect to your argument.”

“Long quote here, but no explanation in your own words.”

Voice & Reflection

“This is summary, not analysis — where’s your voice?”

“I don’t see what you think about this.”

“Bring in your own example to make the point clearer.”

“Don’t just list authors — connect them to your case.”

Structure & Coherence

“This sentence doesn’t make sense — rewrite clearly.”

“Right now it’s just a set of words, not a clear idea.”

“You’re jumping between ideas without a link.”

“End with a concluding sentence that ties it all back.”

“Simplify: use clear nouns and straightforward examples.”

Actionable Pushes

“You need a direct quote here with page number.”

“Clarify what you mean by X.”

“Tie this back to your main argument.”

“Be specific: name people, places, or concepts, not vague terms.”

“Cut this down — too much text, not enough clarity.”
